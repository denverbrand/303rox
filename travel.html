Getting around

DIA → venue → wherever else
DIA → Hotel
Rideshare (Uber/Lyft): $35–50, ~30 min to RiNo.
A Line train: $10.50, ~37 min to Union Station, then 10-min rideshare to RiNo. Cheaper, slower.
Rental car: useful if you're road-tripping to the mountains. Pricey to park in RiNo.
To venue
From RiNo: 5–10 min rideshare, ~$10. Drive-yourself works too — parking is on-site.
From downtown: 10–15 min by car. Don't try to walk.
Race morning
Rideshare beats parking stress. Order your ride 2 hrs before your wave — surge pricing kicks in if everyone calls at the same time.
Around town
RTD light rail covers downtown well. RiNo is walkable. For Red Rocks, mountains, or breweries spread across town — Uber or rent a car.

Travel tips

  1. Land in the afternoon if possible. You'll sleep better than if you arrive late and crash dehydrated.
  2. Denver winters can be 65°F and sunny OR snowing. Layer. Pack a warm jacket regardless.
  3. The sun is intense at altitude. SPF 30+ even in November, especially if you're outside more than 30 min.
  4. Tipping is standard US — 18–20% at restaurants, $1–2 per drink at bars.
  5. Carry cash for parking attendants and small vendors. Most places take cards but not all.
  6. Denver is safe + walkable in RiNo, LoDo, Highlands. Be normal urban-aware after dark.

Eat & drink

Pre-race fuel, post-race victory

Pre-race

Familiar food, simple ingredients. Don't experiment 24 hrs before a race.

  • Snooze — popular brunch chain, reliable for pancakes/eggs
  • Jelly Café — solid breakfast/lunch spot in RiNo
  • Sam's No. 3 — diner classics, big portions, late hours
  • The Pig & the Sprout — clean American food, RiNo
  • Whole Foods or Trader Joe's — buy your own race-morning food

Post-race victory

You earned it. Denver's food scene punches above its weight.

  • Work & Class — Latin-inspired small plates, RiNo
  • Hop Alley — modern Chinese, one of Denver's best
  • Safta — modern Israeli/Middle Eastern at The Source
  • Mister Oso — Mexican coastal, vibey RiNo spot
  • Chubby's — late-night green chile burritos, Denver legend

Denver beer scene

Denver is one of the top craft beer cities in the US. Save it for AFTER your race. Pacing tip: pick 2–3 breweries in walking distance vs. trying to hit ten.

  • Great Divide Brewing — OG Denver, two locations (RiNo + Five Points)
  • Ratio Beerworks — RiNo staple, big patio, food on-site
  • Bierstadt Lagerhaus — German-style, the slow-pour pils is famous
  • Black Sky Brewery — pizza + craft beer combo
  • Cerveceria Colorado — Mexican-style beers, attached to Denver Beer Co
  • Bonus: Denver Beer Trail map for the full ride

Altitude survival

The variable that wrecks first-timers

What altitude does to you

At 5,280 ft your body gets ~17% less oxygen than at sea level. Heart rate climbs 5–10 bpm at any given effort. Recovery between efforts takes longer. Hydration loss accelerates 30–40%.

  • You'll feel winded faster on the run leg, especially first 1–2 km
  • Sleep is disrupted the first 1–2 nights — vivid dreams, waking often
  • Mild headaches are common day 1 — usually dehydration, not "real" altitude sickness
  • Your urine should stay light yellow — if it's dark, drink more

How to handle it

No magic fix, but smart prep gets you 80% of the way there. Sea-level athletes have raced sub-90 at altitude before — it's doable.

  • Arrive 2–3 days early if you can. Acclimation curve starts immediately.
  • Hydrate aggressively — 80–100 oz/day + electrolytes 1–2× daily
  • Limit alcohol the 24–48 hrs pre-race. Dehydration multiplier.
  • Cap caffeine at your normal dose — altitude amplifies stimulants
  • Sleep more — 8+ hrs both nights pre-race. Your body's working overtime to adapt.
  • Don't redline early on race day — you'll pay for it on the run leg. Start conservative.
  • If you get a real headache + nausea, take it seriously. Hydrate, rest, descend to lower elevation if it persists.
